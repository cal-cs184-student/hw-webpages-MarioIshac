Overview

This homework was about building up geometry processing tools from scratch. Section 1 covered Bezier curves and surfaces (an extension of 1D curves) using de Casteljau's algorithm. Section 2 involved working with the half-edge data structure to flip and split edges, requiring careful pointer management and assignments. At the end, it was all put together for loop subdivision, enabling the progression of a blocky cube into a round smooth texture. A major lesson was to visualize the needed changes before writing vertex processing code.

Section I: Bezier Curves and Surfaces

Part 1: Bezier curves with 1D de Casteljau subdivision

De Casteljau's algorithm evaluates a point on a Bezier curve at parameter t by repeatedly interpolating between adjacent control points. Starting with n+1 control points, each step lerps consecutive pairs: p_i' = (1-t)*p_i + t*p_{i+1}, giving n points. Repeat until one point left for final result.

My evaluateStep function is essentially a for loop that lerps each pair and pushes the result into a new vector. The GUI calls this repeatedly to walk through the subdivision levels.

Part 2: Bezier surfaces with separable 1D de Casteljau

For surfaces, you have an n x n grid of control points and two parameters u and v. The idea is to evaluate each row as a 1D Bezier curve at u (giving one point per row), then evaluate those n points as another Bezier curve at v. That gives you the surface point at (u, v).

I split this into three functions: evaluateStep does one lerp pass on 3D points, evaluate1D calls that in a loop until one point remains, and evaluate runs evaluate1D on each row with u then on the collected results with v.

Section II: Triangle Meshes and Half-Edge Data Structure

Part 3: Area-weighted vertex normals

I walk around all the faces touching the vertex using the twin()->next() pattern. For each non-boundary face I grab the three vertices and take cross product of two edge vectors. The cross product magnitude is proportional to the triangle's area, so naturally acts as an area weight. I sum up these cross products across all incident faces and call unit() to get final area-weighted vertex normal.

Part 4: Edge flip

Before the flip: triangles (a,b,c) and (c,b,d) sharing edge (b,c). After: triangles (a,d,c) and (a,b,d) sharing edge (a,d). Part 5: Edge split

I used a similar approach as the flip part re. making a visual. However, now there is a new vertex m at the midpoint, 6 new halfedges, 3 new edges, and 2 new faces. The two original triangles become four: (a,b,m), (m,b,d), (a,m,c), (c,m,d). Updated all assignments accordingly.

Tested by doing splits, then flips on the split edges, then splits on flipped edges, etc. This kind of interleaved testing reveals issues that do not pop up when conducting one operation in isolation.

Part 6: Loop subdivision for mesh upsampling

Loop subdivision works in two phases: subdivide every triangle into 4 smaller ones, then update vertex positions with a weighted average. Followed recommended order of computing positions first on the coarse mesh, then actual subdivision next.

For old vertices, the new position is (1 - n*u) * original_pos + u * neighbor_sum, where n is the vertex degree and u is 3 / 16 when n = 3, or 3 / (8n) otherwise. For new edge midpoints, the position is 3/8 * (a + b) + 1/8 * (c + d).

Then, every original edge split (with care to avoid modification of newly introduced edges). After each split, new vertex walked around to distinguish cross edges vs. edges along original direction. Then flip every new edge connecting an old vertex to a new one. Finally, commit all new positions.

Correct tagging of `isNew` proved to be the trickiest component.

Sharp corners and edges seem to get smoothened out with greater iteration count (see handle of teapot). Pre-splitting edges near sharpness seems to decrease the rate at which this happens, maintaining the original features there better.

Cube asymmetry: cube.dae has each face cut into two triangles by a single diagonal, following same gradient / direction. This means subdivision will have a directional bias. I believe pre-processing involving pre-splitting alongside the other directional to get 4 triangles per face in a symmetric orientation would solve the problem. After that, subdivision comes out even because the topology is the same in every direction.
